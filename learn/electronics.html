Electrical Engineering Fundamentals

Understanding electrical principles is critical for building reliable data acquisition systems and avoiding signal artefacts that corrupt experimental results.

Electromagnetic Interference (EMI)

EMI is unwanted electrical energy that couples into signal lines from nearby sources — power supplies, motors, fluorescent lighting, or even other instruments on the same bench. In neuroscience recordings, 50/60 Hz mains interference is the most common artefact.

Key Points

  • Use shielded cables and connect shields at one end only to avoid ground loops
  • Route signal cables away from power cables — maintain at least 10 cm separation
  • Use twisted-pair wiring for differential signals to reject common-mode noise
  • Add ferrite beads to power supply leads entering sensitive enclosures
  • Consider a Faraday cage for electrophysiology and low-signal photometry setups

Grounding & Ground Loops

A ground loop occurs when two or more instruments share a ground connection through multiple paths, creating a loop that acts as an antenna for magnetic interference. This manifests as a persistent low-frequency hum in recordings.

Key Points

  • Establish a single-point (star) ground topology for your experimental setup
  • Use optoisolators or isolated USB hubs to break ground loops between instruments and PCs
  • Never daisy-chain ground connections between devices
  • Verify ground integrity with a multimeter — resistance should be <1 Ω

Signal Conditioning

Raw sensor signals are rarely suitable for direct digitisation. Signal conditioning — filtering, amplification, and impedance matching — ensures that the analogue-to-digital converter receives a clean, properly scaled signal within its input range.

Key Points

  • Always apply an anti-aliasing (low-pass) filter before sampling — set the cut-off below half the ADC sample rate
  • Match sensor output impedance to amplifier input impedance for maximum power transfer
  • Use instrumentation amplifiers for differential signals to reject common-mode noise
  • Place decoupling capacitors (100 nF ceramic + 10 µF electrolytic) close to every IC power pin

PCB Design Basics

A poorly laid out PCB can introduce noise, crosstalk, and thermal issues that no amount of firmware can fix. Good PCB practices are essential for any custom electronics in the lab.

Key Points

  • Use an unbroken ground plane on at least one layer — never route signal traces through it
  • Separate analogue and digital ground regions, connecting them at a single point
  • Keep high-speed or noisy traces (clocks, PWM) away from sensitive analogue inputs
  • Use wider traces for power delivery and add thermal relief pads for hand soldering
