Mechanical Design Fundamentals

Mechanical considerations — from vibration isolation to thermal management — directly affect the precision and reproducibility of neuroscience experiments.

Vibration & Isolation

Vibrations from building HVAC systems, nearby equipment, or even footsteps can displace micro-positioned probes by micrometres — enough to lose a single-unit recording or introduce motion artefacts in imaging.

Key Points

  • Mount sensitive experiments on a vibration-isolated optical table (air or passive dampened)
  • Decouple motorised stages with rubber bushings or vibration-absorbing feet
  • Avoid mounting stepper motors directly on the same structure as recording electrodes
  • Characterise vibration spectra with an accelerometer before and after isolation

Thermal Management

Heat generated by LEDs, laser drivers, or enclosed electronics can drift sensor baselines, warp 3D-printed components, and stress solder joints. Thermal design must be considered from the start, not added as an afterthought.

Key Points

  • Calculate power dissipation for every heat-generating component and size heatsinks accordingly
  • Use thermal vias under power components to conduct heat to a ground plane
  • Ensure adequate ventilation in enclosures — convection or forced air as needed
  • Consider the thermal expansion coefficient of materials when combining metal and plastic parts

Material Selection

The choice of material affects rigidity, weight, biocompatibility, and machinability. Selecting the wrong material can lead to parts that flex under load, corrode in contact with saline, or outgas chemicals that harm live tissue.

Key Points

  • Use stainless steel (316L) or titanium for components in contact with biological fluids
  • PLA is adequate for prototyping but deforms above ~55 °C — use PETG or ABS for final parts
  • Aluminium is excellent for heatsinks and structural frames due to its strength-to-weight ratio
  • Avoid brass near saline — it corrodes and releases zinc ions toxic to tissue

Tolerances & Fits

Specifying appropriate tolerances ensures parts fit together as intended. Over-tolerancing increases cost; under-tolerancing leads to loose assemblies and poor repeatability.

Key Points

  • FDM 3D printers typically achieve ±0.2 mm — design clearances accordingly
  • CNC machining can achieve ±0.05 mm for most features
  • Use press-fit (interference) for permanent assemblies, slip-fit for removable ones
  • Always test critical dimensions with a calliper before committing to a full production run
